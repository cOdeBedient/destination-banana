<p id="mwDA"><i id="mwDQ"><b id="mwDg">Musa</b></i> is one of three <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Genus" title="Genus" id="mwDw">genera</a> in the family <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Musaceae" title="Musaceae" id="mwEA">Musaceae</a>. The genus includes 83 species of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant" id="mwEQ">flowering plants</a> producing edible <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Banana" title="Banana" id="mwEg">bananas</a> and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Cooking_banana" title="Cooking banana" id="mwEw">plantains</a>. Though they grow as high as <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Tree" title="Tree" id="mwFA">trees</a>, banana and plantain plants are not <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wood" title="Wood" id="mwFQ">woody</a> and their apparent "<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Plant_stem" title="Plant stem" id="mwFg">stem</a>" is made up of the bases of the huge <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Leaf" title="Leaf" id="mwFw">leaf</a> <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)" id="mwGA">stalks</a>. Thus, they are technically gigantic <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant" id="mwGQ">herbaceous plants</a>. <i id="mwGg">Musa</i> species are used as food plants by the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Larva" title="Larva" id="mwGw">larvae</a> of some <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera" id="mwHA">Lepidoptera</a> species, including the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Giant_leopard_moth" title="Giant leopard moth" id="mwHQ">giant leopard moth</a> and other <i id="mwHg"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Hypercompe" title="Hypercompe" id="mwHw">Hypercompe</a></i> species, including <i id="mwIA"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Hypercompe_albescens" title="Hypercompe albescens" id="mwIQ">H.<span typeof="mw:Entity" id="mwIg"> </span>albescens</a></i> (only recorded on <i id="mwIw">Musa</i>), <i id="mwJA"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Hypercompe_eridanus" title="Hypercompe eridanus" id="mwJQ">H.<span typeof="mw:Entity" id="mwJg"> </span>eridanus</a></i>, and <i id="mwJw"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Hypercompe_icasia" title="Hypercompe icasia" id="mwKA">H.<span typeof="mw:Entity" id="mwKQ"> </span>icasia</a></i>.</p>

<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" data-mw='{"autoGenerated":true}' id="mwKg"/></section><section data-mw-section-id="1" id="mwKw"><h2 id="Description">Description</h2>
<p id="mwLA">Banana plants are among the largest extant herbaceous plants, some reaching up to <span about="#mwt18" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"cvt","href":"./Template:Cvt"},"params":{"1":{"wt":"30"},"2":{"wt":"ft"},"3":{"wt":"m"},"4":{"wt":"0"},"order":{"wt":"flip"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwLQ">9</span><span typeof="mw:Entity" about="#mwt18"> </span><span about="#mwt18">m (30</span><span typeof="mw:Entity" about="#mwt18"> </span><span about="#mwt18">ft)</span> in height or <span about="#mwt19" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"cvt","href":"./Template:Cvt"},"params":{"1":{"wt":"18"},"2":{"wt":"m"},"3":{"wt":"ft"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwLg">18</span><span typeof="mw:Entity" about="#mwt19"> </span><span about="#mwt19">m (59</span><span typeof="mw:Entity" about="#mwt19"> </span><span about="#mwt19">ft)</span> in the case of <i id="mwLw"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Musa_ingens" title="Musa ingens" id="mwMA">Musa ingens</a></i>. The large herb is composed of a modified underground stem (rhizome), a false trunk or pseudostem formed by the basal parts of tightly rolled leaves, a network of roots, and a large flower spike. A single leaf is divided into a leaf sheath, a contracted part called a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)" id="mwMQ">petiole</a>, and a terminal leaf blade. The false trunk is an aggregation of leaf sheaths;<sup about="#mwt21" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Rou_2-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Rou"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-Rou-2" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 2;" id="mwMg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwMw">[2]</span></a></sup> only when the plant is ready to flower does a true stem grow up through the sheath and droop back down.<sup about="#mwt22" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-3" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-3"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-3" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 3;" id="mwNA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwNQ">[3]</span></a></sup> At the end of this stem, a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Peduncle_(botany)" title="Peduncle (botany)" id="mwNg">peduncle</a> forms (with <i id="mwNw">M. ingens</i> having the second-longest peduncle known, exceeded only by <i id="mwOA"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Agave_salmiana" title="Agave salmiana" id="mwOQ">Agave salmiana</a></i>), bearing many female flowers protected by large purple-red bracts. The extension of the stem (the rachis) continues growth downward, where terminal male flowers grow. The leaves originate from a pseudostem and unroll to show a leaf blade with two lamina halves.<sup about="#mwt23" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Rou_2-1" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Rou"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-Rou-2"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-Rou-2" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 2;" id="mwOg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwOw">[2]</span></a></sup> The lamina can be as much as <span about="#mwt20" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"cvt","href":"./Template:Cvt"},"params":{"1":{"wt":"7"},"2":{"wt":"m"},"3":{"wt":"ft"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwPA">7</span><span typeof="mw:Entity" about="#mwt20"> </span><span about="#mwt20">m (23</span><span typeof="mw:Entity" about="#mwt20"> </span><span about="#mwt20">ft)</span> long in the case of <i id="mwPQ">M.<span typeof="mw:Entity" id="mwPg"> </span>acuminata</i> subsp. <i id="mwPw">truncata</i> (syn. <i id="mwQA">M.<span typeof="mw:Entity" id="mwQQ"> </span>truncata</i>) of the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula" id="mwQg">Malay Peninsula</a>).<sup about="#mwt27" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-4" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-4"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-4" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 4;" id="mwQw"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwRA">[4]</span></a></sup> <i id="mwRQ">Musa</i> species reproduce by both sexual (seed) and asexual (suckers) processes, using asexual means when producing sterile (unseeded) fruits. Further qualities to distinguish <i id="mwRg">Musa</i> include spirally arranged leaves, fruits as berries, the presence of latex-producing cells, flowers with five connate tepals and one member of the inner whorl distinct, and a petiole with one row of air channels.<sup about="#mwt28" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-5" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-5"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-5" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 5;" id="mwRw"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwSA">[5]</span></a></sup></p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="2" id="mwSQ"><h2 id="Distribution">Distribution</h2>
<p id="mwSg">The native distribution of the genus <i id="mwSw">Musa</i> includes most of the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Indomalayan_realm" title="Indomalayan realm" id="mwTA">Indomalayan realm</a> and parts of north-eastern <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Australasian_realm" title="Australasian realm" id="mwTQ">Australasia</a>. It has been introduced to many other parts of the world with tropical or subtropical climates.<sup about="#mwt33" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-powo_6-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"powo"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-powo-6"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-powo-6" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 6;" id="mwTg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwTw">[6]</span></a></sup></p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="3" id="mwUA"><h2 id="Systematics_and_taxonomy">Systematics and taxonomy</h2>

<section data-mw-section-id="4" id="mwUQ"><h3 id="History">History</h3>
<p id="mwUg"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin" id="mwUw">Classical Latin</a> briefly had a name for bananas, namely <a rel="mw:WikiLink/Interwiki" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ariena#Latin" title="wikt:ariena" class="extiw" id="mwVA"><i id="mwVQ">ariena</i></a>, used by <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder" id="mwVg">Pliny the Elder</a> (<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)" id="mwVw"><i id="mwWA">Natural History</i></a>, <a rel="mw:ExtLink nofollow" href="https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.02.0137%3Abook%3D12%3Achapter%3D12" class="external text" id="mwWQ">XII.12</a>). According to <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt" id="mwWg">Alexander von Humboldt</a>,<sup about="#mwt40" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-7" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-7"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-7" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 7;" id="mwWw"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwXA">[7]</span></a></sup> that term may have been a borrowing from <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit" id="mwXQ">Sanskrit</a> <i id="mwXg">varana</i><link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:Wikipedia_articles_needing_clarification_from_March_2024" about="#mwt35" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"Clarify","href":"./Template:Clarify"},"params":{"date":{"wt":"March 2024"},"reason":{"wt":"Does not exist in Sanskrit"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwXw"/><sup class="noprint Inline-Template " style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;" about="#mwt35" id="mwYA"><span typeof="mw:Entity">[</span><i><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Does not exist in Sanskrit (March 2024)">clarification needed</span></a></i><span typeof="mw:Entity">]</span></sup> via Greek rendition <i id="mwYQ">ouarana</i><link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:Wikipedia_articles_needing_clarification_from_March_2024" about="#mwt36" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"Clarify","href":"./Template:Clarify"},"params":{"date":{"wt":"March 2024"},"reason":{"wt":"Does not exist in Greek"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwYg"/><sup class="noprint Inline-Template " style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;" about="#mwt36" id="mwYw"><span typeof="mw:Entity">[</span><i><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Does not exist in Greek (March 2024)">clarification needed</span></a></i><span typeof="mw:Entity">]</span></sup>, following the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Indian_campaign_of_Alexander_the_Great" title="Indian campaign of Alexander the Great" id="mwZA">Indian campaign of Alexander the Great</a>.</p>

<p id="mwZQ">Due to the rarity of bananas in the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" id="mwZg">Late Antiquity</a> and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages" id="mwZw">early Middle Ages</a>, the term <i id="mwaA">ariena</i> faded out of use. During the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages" id="mwaQ">late Middle Ages</a>, international trade brought bananas to Europe, which created the need for a name. In the <span about="#mwt43" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"century","href":"./Template:Century"},"params":{"1":{"wt":"11"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwag">1</span>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin" id="mwaw">Medieval Latin</a> innovated a term <i id="mwbA">musa</i>: this was most likely<sup about="#mwt47" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-8" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"group":"Note"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-8"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-8" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 1;" data-mw-group="Note" id="mwbQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwbg">[Note 1]</span></a></sup> the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Latinisation_of_names" title="Latinisation of names" id="mwbw">latinization</a> of the Arabic name for the fruit, <i id="mwcA">mauz</i> (موز). Thus, the 11th-century Arabic encyclopedia <i id="mwcQ"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./The_Canon_of_Medicine" title="The Canon of Medicine" id="mwcg">The Canon of Medicine</a></i>, which was translated to Latin in medieval times and well known in Europe, shows a correspondence between Arabic <i id="mwcw">mauz</i> and Latin <i id="mwdA">musa</i>.<sup about="#mwt48" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Note1_9-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"group":"Note","name":"Note1"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-Note1-9" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 2;" data-mw-group="Note" id="mwdQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwdg">[Note 2]</span></a></sup> <i id="mwdw">Muz</i> is also the Turkish, Persian, and Somali name for the fruit. </p>

<p id="mweA">According to linguist <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Mark_Donohue_(linguist)" title="Mark Donohue (linguist)" id="mweQ">Mark Donohue</a> and archaeologist Tim Denham, the ultimate origin of the Latinized form <i id="mweg">musa</i> is in the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Trans–New_Guinea_languages" title="Trans–New Guinea languages" id="mwew">Trans–New Guinea languages</a>, where certain cultivars of bananas are known under a form *<i id="mwfA">muku</i>.<sup about="#mwt55" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-DD1_10-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"DD1"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-DD1-10"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-DD1-10" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 8;" id="mwfQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwfg">[8]</span></a></sup> From there, the term was borrowed into the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages" id="mwfw">Austronesian languages</a> of the area, and migrated across Asia, via the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages" id="mwgA">Dravidian languages</a> of India, into <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Persian_language" title="Persian language" id="mwgQ">Persian</a>, Greek, and Arabic as a <i id="mwgg"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wanderwort" title="Wanderwort" id="mwgw">Wanderwort</a></i>:<sup about="#mwt59" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-DD2_11-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"DD2"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-DD2-11"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-DD2-11" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 9;" id="mwhA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwhQ">[9]</span></a></sup><sup about="#mwt63" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-12" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-12"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-12" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 10;" id="mwhg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwhw">[10]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwoQ">The late Latin term <i id="mwog">musa</i> was later chosen by <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus" id="mwow">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1753, as the name for the genus.<sup about="#mwt66" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-WCSP_254737_13-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"WCSP_254737"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-WCSP_254737-13" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 11;" id="mwpA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwpQ">[11]</span></a></sup></p> 

<p id="mwpg">From the time of Linnaeus until the 1940s, different types of edible <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Banana" title="Banana" id="mwpw">bananas</a> and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Cooking_banana" title="Cooking banana" id="mwqA">plantains</a> were given Linnaean binomial names, such as <i id="mwqQ">Musa cavendishii</i>, as if they were species. In fact, edible bananas have an extremely complicated origin involving <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)" id="mwqg">hybridization</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Mutation" title="Mutation" id="mwqw">mutation</a>, and finally <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding" id="mwrA">selection</a> by humans. Most edible bananas are seedless (<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Parthenocarpic" title="Parthenocarpic" class="mw-redirect" id="mwrQ">parthenocarpic</a>), hence sterile, so they are propagated vegetatively. The giving of species names to what are actually very complex, largely asexual, hybrids (mostly of two species of wild bananas, <i id="mwrg"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Musa_acuminata" title="Musa acuminata" id="mwrw">Musa acuminata</a></i> and <i id="mwsA"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Musa_balbisiana" title="Musa balbisiana" id="mwsQ">Musa balbisiana</a></i>) led to endless confusion in banana <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Botany" title="Botany" id="mwsg">botany</a>. In the 1940s and 1950s, it became clear to botanists that the cultivated bananas and plantains could not usefully be assigned Linnean binomials, but were better given <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Cultivar" title="Cultivar" id="mwsw">cultivar</a> names.<link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:All_articles_with_unsourced_statements" about="#mwt67" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"citation needed","href":"./Template:Citation_needed"},"params":{"date":{"wt":"January 2013"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwtA"/><link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:Articles_with_unsourced_statements_from_January_2013" about="#mwt67"/><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;" about="#mwt67" id="mwtQ"><span typeof="mw:Entity">[</span><i><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i><span typeof="mw:Entity">]</span></sup></p>

<p id="mwug">As for the word <i id="mwuw"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Banana" title="Banana" id="mwvA">banana</a></i>, it came to English from Spanish and Portuguese, which had apparently obtained it from a West African language, possibly <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wolof_language" title="Wolof language" id="mwvQ">Wolof</a> (Senegal).<sup about="#mwt70" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-14" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-14"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-14" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 12;" id="mwvg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwvw">[12]</span></a></sup> <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:var( --color-emphasized, #595959 ); border:1px solid #DDD;" about="#mwt69" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"citation needed span","href":"./Template:Citation_needed_span"},"params":{"date":{"wt":"February 2024"},"text":{"wt":"The Wolof form might itself be a [[loanword]] from Arabic &apos;&apos;banān mawz&apos;&apos; (&lt;&apos;&apos;banān&apos;&apos; &apos;finger&apos;)."}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwwA">The Wolof form might itself be a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Loanword" title="Loanword">loanword</a> from Arabic <i>banān mawz</i> (&lt;<i>banān</i> 'finger').</span><link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:All_articles_with_unsourced_statements" about="#mwt69"/><link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:Articles_with_unsourced_statements_from_February_2024" about="#mwt69"/><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;" about="#mwt69" id="mwwQ"><span typeof="mw:Entity">[</span><i><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. </section><section data-mw-section-id="5" id="mwwg"><h3 id="Sections">Sections</h3>

<p id="mwww"><i id="mwxA">Musa</i> sections have a history dating back to 1887, when M.P. Sagot published "Sur le genre Bananier", where the genus <i id="mwxQ">Musa</i> was first formally classified.<sup about="#mwt75" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Sagot_15-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Sagot"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-Sagot-15"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-Sagot-15" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 13;" id="mwxg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwxw">[13]</span></a></sup> In this article, Sagot arranged the <i id="mwyA">Musa</i> species into three groups, although no section names were assigned to them. The grouping was based on morphological traits, establishing the trio as bananas with fleshy fruit, ornamental bananas with upright inflorescences and bracts that were vibrantly colored, and bananas that were giant in size.</p>

<p id="mwyQ">Five years after Sagot's article, J.G. Baker made the first formal designation of <i id="mwyg">Musa</i> sections. To do so, he named three subgenera that almost paralleled the sections that had been described by Sagot.<sup about="#mwt80" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_16-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Baker"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-Baker-16"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-Baker-16" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 14;" id="mwyw"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwzA">[14]</span></a></sup> These sections were: </p>
<ul id="mwzQ"><li id="mwzg"><i id="mwzw">M</i>. subg. <i id="mw0A">Physocaulis</i> <small id="mw0Q">Baker</small> – defined by a bract with many flowers, inedible fruits, and a bottle-shaped stem</li>
<li id="mw0g"><i id="mw0w">M.</i> subg. <i id="mw1A">Rhodochlamys</i> <small id="mw1Q">Baker</small> – defined by brightly colored bracts with few flowers, usually inedible fruits, and cylindrical stems</li>
<li id="mw1g"><i id="mw1w">M.</i> subg. <i id="mw2A">Eumusa</i> <small id="mw2Q">Baker</small> – defined by green, brown, or dull-violet bracts with many flowers, usually edible fruits, and cylindrical stems.</li></ul>

<p id="mw2g">After this classification, in 1947, Cheeseman reclassified the taxa based on morphological features and chromosome number.<sup about="#mwt85" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-17" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-17"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-17" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 15;" id="mw2w"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mw3A">[15]</span></a></sup> This project proposed four sections: </p>
<ul id="mw3Q"><li id="mw3g"><i id="mw3w">M</i>. sect. <i id="mw4A">Eumusa</i> <small id="mw4Q">Cheesman</small> (2n = 2x = 22)</li>
<li id="mw4g"><i id="mw4w">M</i>. sect. <i id="mw5A">Rhodochlamys</i> <small id="mw5Q">(Baker) Cheesman</small> (2n = 2x = 22)</li>
<li id="mw5g"><i id="mw5w">M</i>. sect. <i id="mw6A">Australimusa</i> <small id="mw6Q">Cheesman</small> (2n = 2x = 20)</li>
<li id="mw6g"><i id="mw6w">M.</i> sect. <i id="mw7A">Callimusa</i> <small id="mw7Q">Cheesman</small> (2n = 2x = 20)</li></ul>

<p id="mw7g">The addition of another <i id="mw7w">Musa</i> section came in 1976 by G.C.G. Ardent. The added section, <i id="mw8A">M</i>. sect. <i id="mw8Q">Ingentimusa</i>, <small id="mw8g">Ardent</small> was based on a single species, <i id="mw8w">Musa ingens</i>.<sup about="#mwt87" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-18" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-18"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-18" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 16;" id="mw9A"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mw9Q">[16]</span></a></sup> This designation put the number of sections in <i id="mw9g">Musa</i> at five: <i id="mw9w">Eumusa</i>, <i id="mw-A">Rhodochlamys</i>, <i id="mw-Q">Callimusa</i>, <i id="mw-g">Australimusa</i>, and <i id="mw-w">Ingentimusa</i>.</p>

<p id="mw_A">In the 21st century, genomics have become cheaper, more efficient, and more accurate, and <i id="mw_Q">Musa</i> genetic research has increased exponentially. Research was conducted around a diversity of genomic markers (cpDNA, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./NrDNA" title="NrDNA" class="mw-redirect" id="mw_g">nrDNA</a>, rDNA, introns, various spacers, etc.). The results of many of these studies suggested that the five sections of <i id="mw_w">Musa</i> defined by morphology (and listed above) were not monophyletic.<sup about="#mwt90" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-19" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-19"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-19" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 17;" id="mwAQA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwAQE">[17]</span></a></sup><sup about="#mwt94" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Häkkinen-2013_20-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Häkkinen-2013"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-Häkkinen-2013-20"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-Häkkinen-2013-20" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 18;" id="mwAQI"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwAQM">[18]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwAQQ">Based on the incorrect section grouping, Markku Häkkinen proposed another reclassification of the <i id="mwAQU">Musa</i> sections in 2013. Using a multitude of genetic evidence and markers from other studies, Häkkinen suggested the reduction of five <i id="mwAQY">Musa</i> sections into two: <i id="mwAQc">Musa</i> and <i id="mwAQg">Callimusa</i>.<sup about="#mwt96" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Häkkinen-2013_20-1" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Häkkinen-2013"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-Häkkinen-2013-20" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 18;" id="mwAQk"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwAQo">[18]</span></a></sup> Unlike sectional classifications of the past, this hypothesis was based on genetic markers rather than morphological features or chromosome number. The two groups were generally formed by the clustering of the previously defined groups: </p>
<ul id="mwAQs"><li id="mwAQw"><i id="mwAQ0">Musa</i> sect. <i id="mwAQ4">Rhotochlamys</i> and <i id="mwAQ8">M</i>. sect. <i id="mwARA">Eumusa</i><span typeof="mw:Entity" id="mwARE"> </span> became <i id="mwARI">M.</i> sect. <i id="mwARM">Musa</i></li>
<li id="mwARQ"><i id="mwARU">M</i>. sect. <i id="mwARY">Ingetimusa</i>, <i id="mwARc">M</i>. sect. <i id="mwARg">Callimusa</i> and <i id="mwARk">M</i>. sect. <i id="mwARo">Australimusa</i> became <i id="mwARs">M</i>. sect. <i id="mwARw">Callimusa</i></li></ul>

<p id="mwAR0">The advance of genomic analysis technologies and further data on the relatedness of <i id="mwAR4">Musa</i> species, formulated Häkkinen's two sections and later corroborated them as correct subcategories for the genus.<sup about="#mwt103" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-21" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-21"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-21" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 19;" id="mwAR8"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwASA">[19]</span></a></sup><sup about="#mwt107" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-22" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-22"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-22" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 20;" id="mwASE"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwASI">[20]</span></a></sup><sup about="#mwt111" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-23" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-23"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-23" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 21;" id="mwASM"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwASQ">[21]</span></a></sup> The history of <i id="mwASU">Musa</i> sections provides an example of genomics superseding morphological evidence and thus classifications.</p>

</section></section></section><section data-mw-section-id="12" id="mwAyw"><h2 id="Cultivated_bananas">Cultivated bananas</h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" about="#mwt128" typeof="mw:Extension/templatestyles mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"main","href":"./Template:Main"},"params":{"1":{"wt":"Banana"},"2":{"wt":"Cooking banana"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwAy0"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" about="#mwt128" id="mwAy4">Main articles: <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Banana" title="Banana">Banana</a> and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Cooking_banana" title="Cooking banana">Cooking banana</a></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb" id="mwAy8"><a href="./File:Bananavarieties.jpg" class="mw-file-description" id="mwAzA"><img resource="./File:Bananavarieties.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Bananavarieties.jpg/220px-Bananavarieties.jpg" decoding="async" data-file-width="387" data-file-height="221" data-file-type="bitmap" height="126" width="220" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Bananavarieties.jpg/330px-Bananavarieties.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/de/Bananavarieties.jpg 2x" class="mw-file-element" id="mwAzE"/></a><figcaption id="mwAzI">Left to right: <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Plantain_(cooking)" title="Plantain (cooking)" class="mw-redirect" id="mwAzM">plantains</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Red_banana" title="Red banana" id="mwAzQ">red bananas</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Latundan" title="Latundan" class="mw-redirect" id="mwAzU">latundan</a>, and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Cavendish_banana" title="Cavendish banana" id="mwAzY">Cavendish bananas</a></figcaption></figure>
<p id="mwAzc">A number of distinct groups of plants bearing edible fruit have been developed from species of <i id="mwAzg">Musa</i>. In English, fruits which are sweet and used for dessert are usually called "bananas", whereas starchier varieties used for cooking are called "plantains", but these terms do not have any botanical significance. By far the largest and now the most widely distributed group of cultivated bananas is derived from section <i id="mwAzk">Musa</i>, particularly <span class="nowrap" about="#mwt131" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"nobreak","href":"./Template:Nobreak"},"params":{"1":{"wt":"&apos;&apos;M. acuminata&apos;&apos;"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwAzo"><i>M. acuminata</i></span> and <span class="nowrap" about="#mwt132" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"nobreak","href":"./Template:Nobreak"},"params":{"1":{"wt":"&apos;&apos;M. balbisiana&apos;&apos;"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwAzs"><i>M. balbisiana</i></span>, either alone or in various hybrid combinations. The next but much smaller group is derived from members of section <i id="mwAzw">Callimusa</i> (previously classified as <i id="mwAz0">Australimusa</i>) and is restricted in importance to <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Polynesia" title="Polynesia" id="mwAz4">Polynesia</a>. Of even more restricted importance are small groups of hybrids from <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea" id="mwAz8">Papua New Guinea</a>; a group from section <i id="mwA0A">Musa</i> to which <i id="mwA0E"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Musa_schizocarpa" title="Musa schizocarpa" id="mwA0I">Musa schizocarpa</a></i> has also contributed, and a group of hybrids between section <i id="mwA0M">Musa</i> and section <i id="mwA0Q">Callimusa</i>.<link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:All_articles_with_unsourced_statements" about="#mwt133" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"citation needed","href":"./Template:Citation_needed"},"params":{"date":{"wt":"January 2013"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwA0U"/><link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:Articles_with_unsourced_statements_from_January_2013" about="#mwt133"/><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;" about="#mwt133" id="mwA0Y"><span typeof="mw:Entity">[</span><i><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i><span typeof="mw:Entity">]</span></sup></p>

<p id="mwA0c">Banana and plantains are the fourth most produced food globally surpassed only by the staple crops of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Rice" title="Rice" id="mwA0g">rice</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wheat" title="Wheat" id="mwA0k">wheat</a> and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Maize" title="Maize" id="mwA0o">maize</a>.</p>

<section data-mw-section-id="13" id="mwA0s"><h3 id="Properties">Properties</h3>
<p id="mwA0w">Plants of the <i id="mwA00">Musa</i> spp. including roots, flowers and fruits have been used in the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Folk_medicine" title="Folk medicine" class="mw-redirect" id="mwA04">folk medicine</a> cultures of Africa, Asia, India and the Americas. Modern studies examining the properties of the fruits have found diversity of bioactive compounds among <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Genotypes" title="Genotypes" class="mw-redirect" id="mwA08">genotypes</a> compared with commercially grown <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Cultivars" title="Cultivars" class="mw-redirect" id="mwA1A">cultivars</a>.<sup about="#mwt138" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-31" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-31"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-31" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 27;" id="mwA1E"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwA1I">[27]</span></a></sup></p>
 
</section><section data-mw-section-id="14" id="mwA1M"><h3 id="Section_Musa_cultivars">Section <i id="mwA1Q">Musa</i> cultivars</h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" about="#mwt139" typeof="mw:Extension/templatestyles mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"Further","href":"./Template:Further"},"params":{"1":{"wt":"List of banana cultivars"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwA1U"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" about="#mwt139" id="mwA1Y">Further information: <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./List_of_banana_cultivars" title="List of banana cultivars">List of banana cultivars</a></div>
<p id="mwA1c">When the Linnaean binomial system was abandoned for cultivated bananas, an alternate genome-based system for the nomenclature of edible bananas in section <i id="mwA1g">Musa</i> was devised. Thus, the plant previously known by the "species" name <i id="mwA1k">Musa cavendishii</i> became <i id="mwA1o">Musa</i> (AAA Group) 'Dwarf Cavendish'. The "new" name shows clearly that 'Dwarf Cavendish' is a triploid, with three sets of chromosomes, all derived from <i id="mwA1s">Musa acuminata</i>, which is designated by the letter "A". When <i id="mwA1w">Musa balbisiana</i> is involved, the letter "B" is used to denote its genome. Thus, the cultivar 'Rajapuri' may be called <i id="mwA10">Musa</i> (AAB Group) 'Rajapuri'. 'Rajapuri' is also a triploid, expected to have two sets of chromosomes from <i id="mwA14">Musa acuminata</i> and one from <i id="mwA18">Musa balbisiana</i>. In the genome of edible bananas from section <i id="mwA2A">Musa</i>, combinations such as AA, BB, ABB, BBB and even AAAB can be found.</p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="15" id="mwA2E"><h3 id="Fe'i-type_cultivars"><span id="Fe.27i-type_cultivars" typeof="mw:FallbackId"></span>Fe'i-type cultivars</h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" about="#mwt142" typeof="mw:Extension/templatestyles mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"main","href":"./Template:Main"},"params":{"1":{"wt":"Fe&apos;i banana"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwA2I"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" about="#mwt142" id="mwA2M">Main article: <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Fe'i_banana" title="Fe'i banana">Fe'i banana</a></div>
<p id="mwA2Q">No such nomenclature system has been developed for the group of edible bananas derived from section <i id="mwA2U">Callimusa</i>. This group is known generally as the "Fe'i" or "Fehi" bananas, and numerous cultivars are found in the South Pacific region. They are very distinctive plants with upright fruit bunches, featuring in three of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Paul_Gauguin" title="Paul Gauguin" id="mwA2Y">Paul Gauguin</a>'s paintings. The flesh can be cooked before eating and is bright orange, with a high level of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Beta_carotene" title="Beta carotene" class="mw-redirect" id="mwA2c">beta carotene</a>. Fe'i bananas are no longer very important for food, as imported foods have grown in popularity, although some have ritual significance. Investigations are under way to use the Fe'i <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Karat_banana" title="Karat banana" id="mwA2g">karat bananas</a> (the name derives from "<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Carrot" title="Carrot" id="mwA2k">carrot</a>" due to the intense orange-yellow color of the fruit) in prevention of childhood blindness in <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Pohnpei" title="Pohnpei" id="mwA2o">Pohnpei</a>.<sup about="#mwt146" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Cogh04_32-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Cogh04"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-Cogh04-32" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 28;" id="mwA2s"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwA2w">[28]</span></a></sup> Fe'i bananas probably derive mainly from <i id="mwA20"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Musa_maclayi" title="Musa maclayi" id="mwA24">Musa maclayi</a></i>, although their origins are not as well understood as the section <i id="mwA28">Musa</i> bananas. Cultivars can be formally named, as e.g. <i id="mwA3A">Musa</i> (Fe'i Group) 'Utafun'.</p>

</section></section><section data-mw-section-id="16" id="mwA3E"><h2 id="Other_uses">Other uses</h2>
<p id="mwA3I">In addition to the edible fruits, the flowers can be eaten cooked, and the heart of the plant (like <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Heart_of_palm" title="Heart of palm" id="mwA3M">heart of palm</a>) can be eaten raw or cooked. Additionally, the rootstocks and leaf sheaths of some species can be cooked and eaten.<sup about="#mwt151" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-33" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-33"}}'><a href="./Musa_(genus)#cite_note-33" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 29;" id="mwA3Q"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwA3U">[29]</span></a></sup></p>
