<p id="mwDg">"<b id="mwDw">Daydream Believer</b>" is a song composed by American songwriter <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./John_Stewart_(musician)" title="John Stewart (musician)" id="mwEA">John Stewart</a> shortly before he left <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./The_Kingston_Trio" title="The Kingston Trio" id="mwEQ">The Kingston Trio</a>. It was originally recorded by <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./The_Monkees" title="The Monkees" id="mwEg">the Monkees</a>, with <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Davy_Jones_(musician)" title="Davy Jones (musician)" id="mwEw">Davy Jones</a> singing the lead. The single reached No. 1 on the U.S. <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100" id="mwFA"><i id="mwFQ">Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart in December 1967, remaining there for four weeks, and peaked at No. 5 on the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart" class="mw-redirect" id="mwFg">UK Singles Chart</a>. It was the Monkees' third and last No. 1 hit in the U.S.</p>

<p id="mwFw">In 1979, "Daydream Believer" was recorded by Canadian singer <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Anne_Murray" title="Anne Murray" id="mwGA">Anne Murray</a>, whose version reached No. 3 on the U.S. country singles chart and No. 12 on the <i id="mwGQ">Billboard</i> Hot 100. The song has been recorded by others, including a 1971 version by Stewart.</p>

<p id="mwGg">The song title was featured in the name of the 2000 biopic about the band, <i id="mwGw"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Daydream_Believers:_The_Monkees'_Story" title="Daydream Believers: The Monkees' Story" id="mwHA">Daydream Believers: The Monkees' Story</a></i>.<sup about="#mwt37" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Dewitt_2000_3-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Dewitt 2000"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-Dewitt_2000-3"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-Dewitt_2000-3" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 3;" id="mwHQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwHg">[3]</span></a></sup><sup about="#mwt41" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Boedeker_2000_4-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Boedeker 2000"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-Boedeker_2000-4"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-Boedeker_2000-4" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 4;" id="mwHw"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwIA">[4]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwLA"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./John_Stewart_(musician)" title="John Stewart (musician)" id="mwLQ">John Stewart</a> wrote "Daydream Believer" as the third in a trilogy of songs about suburban life,<sup about="#mwt56" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":":0"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-:0-6"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-:0-6" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 6;" id="mwLg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwLw">[6]</span></a></sup> recalling: "I remember going to bed thinking, 'What a wasted day — all I’ve done is daydream.' And from there I wrote the whole song. I never thought it was one of my best songs. Not at all".<sup about="#mwt60" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-American_Songwriter_7-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"American Songwriter"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-American_Songwriter-7"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-American_Songwriter-7" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 7;" id="mwMA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwMQ">[7]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwMg">The song was turned down by <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./We_Five" title="We Five" id="mwMw">We Five</a> and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Spanky_and_Our_Gang" title="Spanky and Our Gang" id="mwNA">Spanky and Our Gang</a>. While attending a party at <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Hoyt_Axton" title="Hoyt Axton" id="mwNQ">Hoyt Axton</a>'s home in Hollywood's <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Laurel_Canyon" title="Laurel Canyon" class="mw-redirect" id="mwNg">Laurel Canyon</a>, producer <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Chip_Douglas" title="Chip Douglas" id="mwNw">Chip Douglas</a> told Stewart that he was now producing the Monkees and asked if Stewart had any songs that might work for the group. Stewart offered "Daydream Believer."<sup about="#mwt63" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":":0"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-:0-6" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 6;" id="mwOA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwOQ">[6]</span></a></sup> The song was recorded during the sessions for the Monkees' 1967 album <i id="mwOg"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Pisces,_Aquarius,_Capricorn_&amp;_Jones_Ltd." title="Pisces, Aquarius, Capricorn &amp; Jones Ltd." id="mwOw">Pisces, Aquarius, Capricorn &amp; Jones Ltd.</a></i>, but was ultimately included on their 1968 album <i id="mwPA"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./The_Birds,_The_Bees_&amp;_the_Monkees" title="The Birds, The Bees &amp; the Monkees" class="mw-redirect" id="mwPQ">The Birds, The Bees &amp; the Monkees</a></i>. All four Monkees appear on the track; in addition to the lead vocals by Jones, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Michael_Nesmith" title="Michael Nesmith" id="mwPg">Michael Nesmith</a> plays lead guitar, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Peter_Tork" title="Peter Tork" id="mwPw">Peter Tork</a> plays piano and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Micky_Dolenz" title="Micky Dolenz" id="mwQA">Micky Dolenz</a> sings backing vocals. Tork created the piano introduction, and the orchestral arrangement was created by jazz trumpeter and composer <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Shorty_Rogers" title="Shorty Rogers" id="mwQQ">Shorty Rogers</a>, who included the same seven-note phrase preceding the chorus that can be heard on the Beach Boys' "<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Help_Me,_Rhonda" title="Help Me, Rhonda" id="mwQg">Help Me, Rhonda</a>."<link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:All_articles_with_unsourced_statements" about="#mwt62" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"Citation needed","href":"./Template:Citation_needed"},"params":{"reason":{"wt":"The two phrases bear only a passing resemblance. We need a source for this claim."},"date":{"wt":"February 2020"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwQw"/><link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:Articles_with_unsourced_statements_from_February_2020" about="#mwt62"/><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;" about="#mwt62" id="mwRA"><span typeof="mw:Entity">[</span><i><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="The two phrases bear only a passing resemblance. We need a source for this claim. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i><span typeof="mw:Entity">]</span></sup></p>

<p id="mwRQ">Jones claimed he had been "pissed off" recording the song, with his lead vocal showing a hint of annoyance at the ongoing takes.<sup about="#mwt65" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-8" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-8"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-8" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 8;" id="mwRg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwRw">[8]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwSA">According to <i id="mwSQ">Billboard</i> Hot 100 chart historian <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Joel_Whitburn" title="Joel Whitburn" id="mwSg">Joel Whitburn</a>'s mid-1980s book <i id="mwSw">The Billboard Book of Number One Hits</i>, the recording was originally scheduled to be the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./B-side" title="B-side" class="mw-redirect" id="mwTA">B-side</a> of the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Barry_Mann" title="Barry Mann" id="mwTQ">Barry Mann</a>/<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Cynthia_Weil" title="Cynthia Weil" id="mwTg">Cynthia Weil</a> song "Love Is Only Sleeping" (from <i id="mwTw"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Pisces,_Aquarius,_Capricorn_&amp;_Jones_Ltd." title="Pisces, Aquarius, Capricorn &amp; Jones Ltd." id="mwUA">Pisces, Aquarius, Capricorn &amp; Jones Ltd.</a></i>), featuring lead vocals by Michael Nesmith. However, a week before release, it was discovered that the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Europe" title="Europe" id="mwUQ">European</a> single masters for "Love Is Only Sleeping" were not ready, but the masters for "Daydream Believer" were. A last-minute switch meant that "Daydream Believer" now became the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./A-side" title="A-side" class="mw-redirect" id="mwUg">A-side</a> and "<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Goin'_Down_(The_Monkees_song)" title="Goin' Down (The Monkees song)" id="mwUw">Goin' Down</a>," a song written by all four Monkees with <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Diane_Hildebrand" title="Diane Hildebrand" id="mwVA">Diane Hildebrand</a> in the style of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Mose_Allison" title="Mose Allison" id="mwVQ">Mose Allison</a>, became the flip side. Nesmith would not be given a lead vocal on a Monkees single A-side until 1969's "<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Listen_to_the_Band_(song)" title="Listen to the Band (song)" id="mwVg">Listen to the Band</a>." Allegedly, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Colgems_Records" title="Colgems Records" id="mwVw">Colgems Records</a> did not like Nesmith's voice, preferring the voices of both Dolenz and Jones, and was further aggrieved when Nesmith insisted on the inclusion of at least two of his songs per album.<link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:All_articles_with_unsourced_statements" about="#mwt66" typeof="mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"Citation needed","href":"./Template:Citation_needed"},"params":{"reason":{"wt":"The assertion that Colgems would block the release of a Nesmith-vocal single because they disliked his voice appears to be speculative and needs some attribution."},"date":{"wt":"February 2020"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwWA"/><link rel="mw:PageProp/Category" href="./Category:Articles_with_unsourced_statements_from_February_2020" about="#mwt66"/><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;" about="#mwt66" id="mwWQ"><span typeof="mw:Entity">[</span><i><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="The assertion that Colgems would block the release of a Nesmith-vocal single because they disliked his voice appears to be speculative and needs some attribution. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i><span typeof="mw:Entity">]</span></sup> Previously, Nesmith's lead vocal version of "<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./The_Girl_I_Knew_Somewhere" title="The Girl I Knew Somewhere" id="mwWg">The Girl I Knew Somewhere</a>" was replaced with Dolenz on lead vocals for the B-side of the single "<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./A_Little_Bit_Me,_a_Little_Bit_You" title="A Little Bit Me, a Little Bit You" id="mwWw">A Little Bit Me, a Little Bit You</a>".</p>

<p id="mwXA"><i id="mwXQ"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)" id="mwXg">Billboard</a></i> described the single as a "well written easy beat rhythm ballad" with a "clever opening."<sup about="#mwt74" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-bb_9-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"bb"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-bb-9"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-bb-9" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 9;" id="mwXw"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwYA">[9]</span></a></sup>  <i id="mwYQ"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Cash_Box" title="Cash Box" class="mw-redirect" id="mwYg">Cash Box</a></i> said that it has "fascinating <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Arrangement_(music)" title="Arrangement (music)" class="mw-redirect" id="mwYw">arrangements</a> that develop from a simple piano opening to a compelling ork ensemble and the hypnotic repetition of a very catchy <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Refrain" title="Refrain" id="mwZA">refrain</a>."<sup about="#mwt78" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-cb_10-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"cb"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-cb-10"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-cb-10" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 10;" id="mwZQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwZg">[10]</span></a></sup> <i id="mwZw"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Record_World" title="Record World" id="mwaA">Record World</a></i> said "it has a magnetic melody and lyrics."<sup about="#mwt82" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-11" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-11"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-11" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 11;" id="mwaQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwag">[11]</span></a></sup> According to <i id="mwaw"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)" id="mwbA">Variety</a></i>, the song's lyrics focus on the endgame of a comfy but increasingly distant relationship, with the narrator "caught in mid-gaze before the bathroom mirror, reflecting on the quiet dissolution of his materialistic marriage – a union between 'a daydream believer and a homecoming queen,' now curdled, driven more by money than by romance."<sup about="#mwt86" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Variety_12-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Variety"},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-Variety-12"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-Variety-12" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 12;" id="mwbQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwbg">[12]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwbw"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./RCA_Records" title="RCA Records" id="mwcA">RCA Records</a> did not like the song as written by Stewart, and insisted on changing a critical word. Stewart originally wrote: "Now you know how funky I can be," but RCA wanted to change it to "Now you know how happy I can be," as one meaning of "funky" is "smelly." Stewart initially objected because the change would completely reverse the meaning of the line and would not make sense in the context of the song. He relented because RCA was adamant and Stewart realized that the song could be a hit. In 2006, Stewart said that the proceeds from "Daydream Believer" "...kept me alive for all these years."<sup about="#mwt88" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-2" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":":0"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-:0-6" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 6;" id="mwcQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwcg">[6]</span></a></sup></p><!-- Using corrected date and direct quote in Stewart's exact words -->

<p id="mwcw">In 1986, three of the four Monkees (Dolenz, Jones and Tork) mounted a successful reunion tour and had a major hit with the newly recorded "<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./That_Was_Then,_This_Is_Now_(song)" title="That Was Then, This Is Now (song)" id="mwdA">That Was Then, This Is Now</a>." <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Arista_Records" title="Arista Records" id="mwdQ">Arista Records</a>, which owned the Monkees' masters at the time, rereleased "Daydream Believer" as a followup single, remixed with a new and heavier percussion track by <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Michael_Lloyd_(music_producer)" title="Michael Lloyd (music producer)" id="mwdg">Michael Lloyd</a>, who had produced "That Was Then, This Is Now."</p>

<section data-mw-section-id="10" id="mwxA"><h3 id="Background_2">Background</h3>
<p id="mwxQ">Canadian singer <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Anne_Murray" title="Anne Murray" id="mwxg">Anne Murray</a> recorded a cover version of "Daydream Believer" for her <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Platinum_certification" title="Platinum certification" class="mw-redirect" id="mwxw">platinum-certified</a> 1979 studio album <i id="mwyA"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./I'll_Always_Love_You_(album)" title="I'll Always Love You (album)" id="mwyQ">I'll Always Love You</a></i>. Produced by <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Jim_Ed_Norman" title="Jim Ed Norman" id="mwyg">Jim Ed Norman</a> and issued on <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records" id="mwyw">Capitol Records</a> the following year, Murray's single became her eighth No. 1 hit on the U.S. <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Hot_Adult_Contemporary_Tracks" title="Hot Adult Contemporary Tracks" class="mw-redirect" id="mwzA">Adult Contemporary</a> chart.<sup about="#mwt192" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-26" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-26"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-26" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 26;" id="mwzQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwzg">[26]</span></a></sup> It reached No. 12 on the <i id="mwzw">Billboard</i> Hot 100 chart and No. 3 on <i id="mw0A">Billboard'</i>s country chart.<sup about="#mwt196" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-27" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-27"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-27" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 27;" id="mw0Q"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mw0g">[27]</span></a></sup> For her 2007 album <i id="mw0w"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Anne_Murray_Duets:_Friends_and_Legends" title="Anne Murray Duets: Friends and Legends" class="mw-redirect" id="mw1A">Anne Murray Duets: Friends and Legends</a>,</i> Murray rerecorded the song as a duet with <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Nelly_Furtado" title="Nelly Furtado" id="mw1Q">Nelly Furtado</a>.<sup about="#mwt200" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-28" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-28"}}'><a href="./Daydream_Believer#cite_note-28" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 28;" id="mw1g"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mw1w">[28]</span></a></sup>
Cover Version
in 1989, Hong Kong singer Bowie Lam covered this song in Cantonese.</p>
